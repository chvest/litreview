Screening Process

This tool implements a three-stage systematic literature screening workflow following established guidelines for systematic reviews [1]:

  1. Title screening — reviewers assess whether a paper's title (and available metadata) indicates potential relevance. Papers not excluded here advance to abstract review.
  2. Abstract screening — reviewers read the abstract and decide whether the paper meets the inclusion criteria. Papers snowballed from reference lists enter the workflow here, bypassing title screening.
  3. Full-text review — reviewers assess the complete paper against all inclusion and exclusion criteria and record a final decision.

At each stage, papers are advanced to the next stage only when the configured inclusion threshold is met (e.g., any non-excluded reviewer, majority, or all reviewers). Uncertain decisions count as non-excluded and do not block advancement.

Inter-Rater Agreement Metrics

Agreement between reviewers is quantified using three complementary metrics. All three are computed on the subset of papers reviewed by both reviewers in the selected stage, using the three-category scale: include, uncertain, exclude.

Simple Percent Agreement

The proportion of papers on which both reviewers gave identical decisions:

Po = (number of agreements) / (total papers reviewed by both)

Simple and interpretable, but does not correct for the chance agreement that would occur even if both reviewers chose randomly. Can therefore overstate the true level of systematic agreement, particularly when one category dominates.

Cohen's κ (Kappa)

Cohen's κ corrects simple agreement for the expected agreement under chance, computed from each reviewer's individual marginal rates [2]:

κ = (Po − Pe) / (1 − Pe)

where Pe is the expected chance agreement calculated from the product of each reviewer's marginal probabilities for each category.

Prevalence problem. In systematic reviews, the vast majority of papers are excluded. This creates a highly skewed label distribution. Cohen's κ computes Pe from each reviewer's individual marginal rates — when both reviewers exclude ~90% of papers, the formula assigns most of their agreement to chance, driving κ artificially low even when reviewers are highly consistent. Values can legitimately be negative (meaning worse-than-chance agreement) or close to zero even when simple agreement exceeds 90%. For this reason, Gwet's AC1 is preferred for systematic reviews.
Gwet's AC1

Gwet's AC1 addresses the prevalence problem by computing expected chance agreement from averaged marginal probabilities rather than products of individual marginals [3]:

AC1 = (Po − Pe*) / (1 − Pe*)   where   Pe* = Σk π̄k(1 − π̄k) / (K−1)

π̄k is the average marginal probability of category k across both reviewers, and K is the number of categories (3 in this tool). By using averaged rather than individual marginals, AC1 does not inflate expected agreement when categories are imbalanced.

AC1 is recommended for systematic reviews by Wongpakaran et al. [4], and an AC1 ≥ 0.6 is generally considered acceptable for proceeding to full-text review.

Interpretation Scale

Both κ and AC1 are interpreted using the scale of Landis & Koch (1977) [5], originally defined for κ and commonly applied to AC1:

ValueInterpretation
< 0Worse than chance
0.00–0.20Slight
0.21–0.40Fair
0.41–0.60Moderate
0.61–0.80Substantial
0.81–1.00Almost Perfect

Note: Landis & Koch acknowledge these boundaries are arbitrary. McHugh (2012) [6] provides an accessible overview of their practical use.
